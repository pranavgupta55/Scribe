To understand this better, we need to go back to a Polish-American philosopher called Alfred Korzybski. Now, Korzybski is principally famous now for having coined the phrase, the map is not the territory. And it's a useful phrase in my field of behavioral economics, because one of the things we would say is that economics provides you with a map, Reality provides you with a territory. And that what's left out of the map is often more important than the information that the map contains. That economics used on its own gives you a very, very unreliable guide to how humans will behave. And one of the strongest economic assumptions would be if you ask people, do you prefer a plastic bottle or a can? And they say, don't care, because they don't know. And then you say, knowing that a can costs more than the plastic bottle, would you be prepared to pay for it? The people would say, no. And that's probably true as far as it goes.
