# SaaS Misconceptions and Validation

> SaaS requires 7+ years zero profit; existing business with margins/cashflow is better foundation.

## Key Numbers
- Build cost: $500k initial, $120k/year maintenance
- Break-even: 150 customers (current audience has this)
- $2M/year: 2,000 customers
- Profitability: 7+ years, maybe never

## Key Takeaway
- Having existing customer base doesn't guarantee SaaS success
- Sunk-cost fallacy: already paid $500k, so finish it
- Best money to spend = deciding to stop spending bad money

## Common Mistakes
- Founder thinks SaaS = passive recurring revenue, escape hatch from current business
- Confuses building software with building a market
- Underestimates 7+ year burn before profitability

## How-To / Method
- If you have $2.6M revenue + 30% margin growth + $500k/year expense + only need $150 customers to break even = improve current business, not pivot to SaaS
- Solve real constraint first (reach more stylists, increase pricing) before building new product

## Claims
- A hair extension wholesale business with $2.6M revenue, 50% margins on salon ($900k) and 30% month-over-month growth on wholesale is better positioned to scale than pivoting to a $500k-build SaaS project costing $120k/year to maintain. — [helping_e_commerce_business_owners_scale_8C_6qojTA78.txt]
- Building a SaaS company requires 7+ years of zero revenue; only 150 SaaS customers needed to break even, 2,000 to hit $2M/year, but the business won't reach profitability or demonstrate product-market fit for years; this is a sunk-cost-fallacy trap. — [helping_e_commerce_business_owners_scale_8C_6qojTA78.txt]
- Recurring revenue (hair extensions every 8-16 weeks) makes a product naturally scalable; a $2.6M wholesale business doing 30% month-over-month growth with 50% margins is more cashflow-positive and chill than pivoting to unproven SaaS. — [helping_e_commerce_business_owners_scale_8C_6qojTA78.txt]
